Low-Power Two-Step Successive Approximation ADC

This project develops a low-power 10-bit SAR ADC using a hybrid architecture consisting of a 6-bit voltage-mode SAR ADC, a Voltage-to-Time Converter (VTC), and a 4-bit Vernier Time-to-Digital Converter (TDC). Designed for applications in wireless sensors and biomedical instruments, it emphasizes high efficiency and low power consumption.

Project Description

Overview

The Low-Power Two-Step Successive Approximation ADC is a state-of-the-art 10-bit Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC) designed for applications in biomedical sensors and wireless sensor networks. This project achieves high resolution and energy efficiency through a hybrid architecture that integrates a 6-bit Voltage-Mode SAR ADC, a Voltage-to-Time Converter (VTC), and a 4-bit Vernier Time-to-Digital Converter (TDC).

What is an ADC?

An Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC) is an essential component in modern electronics, responsible for converting continuous analog signals (such as voltages, sound waves, or temperatures) into discrete digital signals that can be processed by computers or microcontrollers. ADCs bridge the gap between the physical world and digital systems, enabling applications like medical devices, smartphones, and IoT systems to interpret real-world data.

What is a SAR ADC?

A Successive Approximation Register (SAR) ADC is a popular ADC architecture known for its efficiency and balance of speed, resolution, and power consumption. SAR ADCs work through an iterative process, using a binary search algorithm to narrow down the input signalâ€™s value step by step. Each step compares the input signal to a reference voltage, gradually refining the digital output. This makes SAR ADCs particularly suited for low-power, high-precision applications such as biomedical devices and portable electronics.

What is a Voltage-to-Time Converter (VTC)?

A Voltage-to-Time Converter (VTC) is a specialized circuit that maps a residual voltage (the difference left after a coarse digitization step) into a time delay. In a hybrid ADC, this step enhances resolution without requiring additional power-consuming circuitry. The VTC's ability to work with a small input range makes it a critical component for improving the overall linearity and precision of the ADC.

What is a Time-to-Digital Converter (TDC)?

A Time-to-Digital Converter (TDC) transforms the time delay generated by the VTC into a digital value. Using techniques like Vernier delay lines, the TDC achieves high precision by measuring small differences in time intervals. This step completes the hybrid ADC architecture, converting the time-domain signal into a final digital output. TDCs are valued for their low power consumption and accuracy, making them ideal for high-resolution designs.

How These Components Work Together

The Low-Power Two-Step Successive Approximation ADC integrates:

  • A 6-bit Voltage-Mode SAR ADC for coarse digitization of the input signal.
  • A VTC to convert the residual voltage into a time-domain signal.
  • A 4-bit Vernier TDC to refine the output and achieve the final 10-bit resolution.
This hybrid approach ensures high accuracy, low power consumption, and scalability for applications requiring energy efficiency and precision.

How It Works

Step 1: Analog Input Signal

The ADC begins by receiving an analog signal, such as a sine wave (e.g., a biomedical sensor's voltage output). The front-end circuit amplifies and conditions this signal to prepare it for digitization.

Step 2: Coarse Digitization with SAR ADC

The 6-bit SAR ADC performs coarse digitization using a successive approximation algorithm. It compares the input voltage against a reference voltage, generating a 6-bit approximation of the input signal.

Step 3: Residual Voltage

The SAR ADC generates a residual voltage representing the difference between the input signal and the coarse approximation. This signal is key to achieving high precision.

Step 4: Voltage-to-Time Conversion (VTC)

The VTC maps the residual voltage into a precise time delay. Smaller voltages result in shorter delays, while larger voltages create longer delays.

Step 5: Fine Digitization with Vernier TDC

The 4-bit Vernier TDC digitizes the time delay with sub-nanosecond precision, using two delay lines (fast and slow) to measure time differences accurately.

Step 6: Digital Output

The ADC outputs a 10-bit digital signal, combining the SAR ADC's 6 bits and the Vernier TDC's 4 bits, to precisely represent the original analog input.
